The G2 Road Test Scoring Sheet – What Every Student Needs to Know

Published on July 23, 2024

DriveTest examiners use an examination sheet to score candidates during their road tests. While the G2 and G test sheets are similar, the G test sheet includes additional maneuvers for assessment, such as highway driving and since 2022 they've stopped testing for road-side stops, 3 point turns and parallel parking. Let's break down the categories of the G2 examiner sheet and outline the grading structure to help you better prepare for your road test and interpret your results afterwards.

Understanding the Scoring System

Before diving into each category, let's look at how examiners mark the sheet and what each symbol means:

  • [OK] Maneuver was satisfactory
  • [✔] Minor error, skill needs improvement (-2 points)
  • [X] Major error, lack of skill, knowledge or judgment (-4 points)
  • [R] Unsafe action at a Railroad Crossing
  • [//] Maneuver not tested

To pass, you need a minimum score of 70/100, which means you can’t lose more than 30 points. One unsafe action (like rolling onto a railway crossing) may result in instant failure.

Key Categories on the G2 Test

1. Vehicle Start-Up

Your test technically begins before you even leave the parking spot. Examiners will check how confidently you:

  • Identify and operate all major safety devices (indicators, brake lights, etc.)
  • Shift into the right gear
  • Signal properly
  • Check mirrors and blindspots before moving
  • Pull out smoothly and in control

Pro tip: Practice this routine in your driveway or a parking lot until it feels automatic.

2. Backing and 3-Point Turns

New drivers must show control while reversing. Most examiners test this with a 3-point turn on a quiet street. What they look for:

  • Mirror and blindspot checks before every adjustment
  • Smooth gear changes when moving into reverse
  • Awareness of what’s behind the car when backing up

Tip: Don’t rush this maneuver. Slow, steady corrections are better than quick, jerky movements.

3. Driving with Traffic

This makes up the bulk of your exam. While driving alongside other cars, examiners pay attention to:

  • Lane changes and passing
  • Right-of-way decisions
  • Mirror checks every 5–7 seconds
  • Blindspot checks before lane changes
  • Speed control and safe following distance

Consistency is key: smooth lane changes and steady speed show you’re in full control.

4. Intersections and Railroad Crossings

You’ll face both controlled and uncontrolled intersections. The examiner scores you on:

  • Proper stops at crosswalks and stop signs
  • Smooth, early braking before intersections
  • Obeying lights and traffic signs
  • Yielding to vehicles with right-of-way

Rolling stops are one of the most common ways students lose points. Always stop fully behind the line.

5. Turns

Turns are graded on:

  • Timing and use of signals
  • Lane positioning
  • Speed and steering control
  • Right-of-way awareness

Failing to check your blindspot before turning is treated as a major error. Practice a mix of left and right turns at different types of intersections so you’re ready for anything.

6. Parking

Parallel parking still makes students nervous, but examiners don’t expect perfection. They mainly want to see that you:

  • Signal before parking
  • Check surroundings for safety
  • Position the car correctly
  • Avoid hitting vehicles or curbs

Touching the curb lightly usually results in a minor deduction, not an automatic failure.

7. Stopping and Starting on a Hill

When asked to park on an incline, examiners watch for:

  • Proper wheel direction (uphill vs. downhill)
  • Correct use of the parking brake

This test shows you can keep the car secure on slopes.

Tips for Success on Test Day

  1. Know the area: practice around neighborhoods and intersections near the testing center.
  2. Drive defensively: anticipate what other drivers may do.
  3. Stay calm: nervousness is normal — focus on one task at a time.
  4. Ask for clarification: if an instruction isn’t clear, ask politely.
  5. Check your vehicle: ensure signals, lights, and brakes work before the test.

Final Thoughts

Making a mistake doesn’t always mean failure. What matters most is showing safe, consistent driving across the whole test. By practicing each of these categories and building strong habits, you’ll walk into your G2 exam with confidence.
