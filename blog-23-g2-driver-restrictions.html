Ontario G2 Licence Restrictions: Key Rules & Safety Tips

Published on October 24, 2025

Driving in Ontario with a G2 license is an exciting step toward more freedom, but it comes with important rules and restrictions to keep you and others safe. Did you know that young drivers in Ontario are more likely to be in accidents? In fact, drivers aged 16-24 are involved in almost 30% of crashes, according to the Ministry of Transportation. These G2 license restrictions are there to help keep you safe and teach you how to be a responsible driver.

Ontario G2 Licence Restrictions

As an Ontario G2 driver, restrictions will be applied to your license, so it's important to understand them. These rules are designed to ensure your safety and the safety of others on the road. Let's break down the key restrictions into two main categories:

Impairment

Zero tolerance for alcohol and cannabis while driving.

Passengers

Age-based restrictions and seatbelt requirements.

Impairment Restrictions

Impairment restrictions for Ontario G2 license include:

Zero Blood Alcohol Level

G2 drivers must maintain a zero blood alcohol level at all times while driving. Consuming alcohol before or while driving can result in severe penalties and legal consequences.

No Cannabis in Your System

Driving with any cannabis in your system is strictly prohibited. These impairment rules align with Ontario's efforts to promote safe and responsible driving practices.

Passenger Restrictions

Passenger restrictions for Ontario G2 license include:

Seatbelt Requirements

As a G2 licence holder, you are responsible for ensuring that all passengers wear working seatbelts. Additionally, you cannot carry more passengers than there are working seatbelts in your vehicle.

Nighttime Passenger Limits for Drivers Under 19

If you're 19 years old or younger, G2 driver limitations are the following:

  • For the first six months of having your G2 License, you can carry only one passenger aged 19 or under between midnight and 5 a.m.
  • After six months, you may carry up to three passengers aged 19 or under during these nighttime hours.

These restrictions do not apply if:

  • A fully licensed G driver with at least 4 years of driving experience (including G2 experience) and a blood alcohol level below 0.05 (or zero if they're under 21) is in the front passenger seat.
  • The passengers aged 19 or under are immediate family members (e.g., siblings, spouses, or parents).
Safety First: While these nighttime driving restrictions may seem limiting, they keep everyone safe on the road. As a G2 driver, you're still gaining experience, and these measures help reduce risks associated with distracted driving, impairment, and overloading vehicles.

What Happens if You Violate G2 Licence Restrictions or Traffic Laws?

As a G2 driver in Ontario, following licence restrictions and traffic laws is crucial. Violating these rules can lead to serious consequences, including fines, demerit points, licence suspensions, or even revocation. Since G2 drivers are considered novices, the penalties for breaking the rules are stricter compared to fully licenced G drivers.

Consequences of Violating Traffic Laws

Violations can lead to the following outcomes:

Tickets and Fines

Every traffic law violation comes with a monetary penalty. The amount depends on the severity of the offence.

Demerit Points

G2 drivers are allowed a maximum of 9 demerit points before their licence is suspended for 60 days. Fully licenced G drivers have more leniency, with a limit of 15 points.

Licence Suspension or Revocation

Severe or repeated offences can lead to longer suspensions. In extreme cases, you may lose your licence and be required to restart the graduated licensing process.

Increased Insurance Costs

Convictions for traffic violations can significantly increase your car insurance premiums, as G2 drivers are already seen as higher-risk drivers.

Penalties for Violating G2 License Restrictions

If you violate the G2 license restrictions, you will be charged with penalties:

Breaking Licence Restrictions

Violations such as carrying too many passengers or driving outside allowed hours may result in immediate fines or suspension.

Committing Serious Offences

Serious violations, such as speeding 30 km/h over the limit or careless driving (worth 4 or more demerit points), attract harsher penalties, including licence suspension.

Zero Tolerance for Alcohol and Cannabis

G2 drivers must adhere to a zero tolerance policy for alcohol and cannabis. Consequences for violations include:

Roadside Suspension
  • 3 days (first offence)
  • 7 days (second offence)
  • 30 days (third offence)
Fines
  • $250 – first offence
  • $350 – second offence
  • $450 – third offence
If Convicted
  • 1st → 30-day suspension
  • 2nd → 90-day suspension
  • 3rd → Licence revoked
Additional consequences: Vehicle towed or impounded if no sober driver is available.

What You Can Do with a G2 License?

Upon passing, drivers receive their G2 licence, which activates the following privileges and restrictions:

Driving Alone

You can now drive alone and there's no need for a fully licensed driver to supervise you from the front passenger seat.

Highway Driving

Unlike G1 licence holders, G2 licence holders are allowed to drive on expressways and 400-series highways where the speed limit is often high.

International Driving Permit

With a valid G2 licence, you can apply for an International Driving Permit (IDP), which allows you to drive in many countries that require one. (Before you do, however, you should carefully consider the risks of driving in foreign countries with your relatively limited driving experience.)

Seatbelts

Like a G1 licence holder, a G2 licence holder must ensure that every passenger has a working seatbelt.

Important Restrictions to Remember

Zero Alcohol

Like a G1 licence holder, you must have a blood alcohol level of 0% whenever you get behind the wheel.

Cell Phones

In Ontario, it is illegal for any driver to use a hand-held cell phone or other hand-held electronic device while driving. However, the penalties for G1 and G2 drivers are harsher, including a 30-day licence suspension for a first offence in addition to the same fine as for fully licensed G drivers.

Demerit Points

A driver's G2 licence will be suspended if the driver accumulates 9 or more demerit points within a two-year period, compared with 15 or more demerit points for a fully licensed G driver. (Nine points can be the equivalent of just two speeding tickets, depending on the type of road and the speeds attained.)

Special Restriction for Young Drivers

If a G2 driver is 19 years old or younger, they can only have one passenger aged 19 or younger between midnight and 5 a.m. This restriction is modified after six months of holding the G2 licence, allowing them to carry up to three passengers under 19 years of age. If the passengers are immediate family members or if the G2 driver is supervised by a fully licensed driver, this restriction is lifted.

This step toward independence is exciting, but it also comes with responsibilities.

The Role of Insurance

As a G2 driver, you're now responsible for your own insurance. Unlike G1 drivers, who rely on their supervisor's coverage, you'll need to be added to a policy or get your own.

Important: If you're driving someone else's car, be extra careful. Any accident or claim will impact their insurance rates.

Planning to buy your own car? Make sure to check insurance costs before you decide. Premiums depend on the car's type and features. Comparing quotes can help you find the best deal and avoid surprises.

As a G2 driver, your insurance costs are usually higher because you're still considered a new driver with less experience. Insurance companies see young drivers as higher risk. However, rates often drop once you gain more experience and turn 25.

Tips to Save on Insurance as a G2 Driver

Take a Driving Course

Completing a government-approved program can lower your premiums.

Maintain Good Grades

Some insurance providers offer discounts to students with excellent academic performance.

Shop Around: Comparing multiple quotes ensures you get the best coverage at an affordable price.

The Freedom of a Full G Licence

After holding a G2 licence for at least 12 months, student drivers can take the G2 exit test. This exam is similar to the G1 exit test, but it will evaluate your advanced driving skills. At this point, you have been practising driving for at least 20 months, which is enough time to gain a lot of experience.

Preparing for the G Licence Test

The G road test checks your ability to drive on highways and other more advanced skills. If you're not ready or lack enough highway experience, take more time to practice before booking your test.

Key Things to Know:

Practice Regularly:

The G test includes everything from the G2 test but adds highway driving and other skills like lane changes and merging. Practice often to feel confident.

Retakes:

If you don't pass, you can retake the test, but each attempt comes with a fee.

Licence Expiry:

If your G2 licence expires before passing the G test, you'll need to restart the process, including the G1 knowledge test.

More Freedom:

After passing the G test, you can drive without restrictions, unless you're under 21. If you're under 21, your blood alcohol and cannabis levels must still be zero while driving.

Tips for Success:

  • Book your test when you feel ready.
  • Get comfortable driving in all conditions, especially on highways.
  • Focus on gaining highway driving experience.
Congratulations! Once you pass the G test, you're officially a fully licensed driver. Enjoy the freedom of driving with no restrictions and stay safe on the road!

Full G Licence Privileges

Once you pass this last driving exam, you will receive a full G licence, which has all the same privileges as a G2 licence and lifts the G2 restrictions. Here's what changes:

Demerit Points

You can accumulate up to 14 demerit points in two years without losing your G licence (compared to 9 points for G2 drivers).

Blood Alcohol Level

The limit on blood alcohol levels increases from 0% to 0.05%, but those under the age of 21 are still required to have zero blood alcohol.

Passenger Safety Responsibilities

Fully licensed drivers must ensure that every passenger under 16 years of age is using a seatbelt or suitable child restraint. Passengers 16 years of age or older are responsible for their own safety.

Professional Opportunities

Full G licence owners can supervise G1 drivers and even pursue professional driving careers, such as becoming a driving instructor or examiner.
